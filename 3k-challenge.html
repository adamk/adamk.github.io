About this series

This series documents the growth of a smaller account in real time using the Foxchase framework. The goal is aggressive account growth, but not reckless execution. In a small account, protecting capital, sizing selectively, and avoiding unnecessary damage matter just as much as pressing good setups.

How this is approached

  • The goal is not “conservative” returns. The goal is strong compounding with controlled risk.
  • Small accounts have less flexibility with settled funds, so mistakes hurt more.
  • Lower-conviction trades deserve less size.
  • The point is not to squeeze every last dollar out of every move if that increases the chance of giving gains back.

Setup Quality & Deployment Framework

Not every trade deserves the same size. Within the Foxchase framework, deployment should scale with setup quality, supporting context, and overall conviction.

Before You Read This Series

This page shows what real discretionary trading is. Depending on market conditions, progress can be uneven, frustrating, and sometimes very choppy. The biggest separator is not finding a magical setup every day. It is risk management.

The $3,000 starting balance was selected by the Foxchase Discord community. In a poll of members, $3,000 was the most common account balance, so the series was designed around a realistic starting point that represented the largest group of participants.

The clearest example of poor risk management in this series was Day 5. After that, stricter bracket orders for more disciplined stop-loss execution were implemented. From there, the curve stabilized and eventually broke out higher. That does not mean every day became easy. It means the downside became more controlled.

Being flat or near breakeven over a month, or even over multiple months, does not automatically mean a trader is unprofitable. Trading strategies often go through choppy periods before conditions become favorable again. What matters is surviving those stretches with enough discipline and capital intact to participate when the better periods arrive.

The goal of this challenge is $25,000. That represents the transition from a true small account into a more flexible account base, where the same framework can be traded with a smaller percentage of total equity at risk. At that point, the goal is no longer small-account aggression for its own sake. The goal becomes more normal sizing, better flexibility, and eventually the ability to pull profits more regularly as income.

Longer term, the purpose of the small account is not just to grow it for its own sake. It is to build enough capital to transition into a more durable structure, including more consistent discretionary sizing and, over time, perhaps allocation toward a passive income options-selling algo.
